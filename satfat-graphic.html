01
The Response-to-Retention Pathway: How Atherosclerosis Actually Starts
Williams & Tabas 1995 · EAS Consensus 2020 · Borén et al., Nat Rev Cardiol 2024

The core mechanistic model is not the "lipid hypothesis" of popular discourse. It is the response-to-retention hypothesis — first articulated in 1995, now the consensus framework supported by direct human pathology, in vitro binding data, and genetic evidence. Inflammation is not a parallel cause; it is a downstream response to particle retention. The sequence is unambiguous in its directionality.

1
Entry via Transcytosis
ApoB-containing lipoproteins (LDL, IDL, VLDL remnants, Lp(a)) enter the subendothelial space through caveolin-1-dependent transcytosis. Entry rate is proportional to circulating particle concentration. Very large particles (>500nm chylomicrons) cannot cross; this size threshold explains why intact VLDL is less atherogenic than its remnants.
2
Retention by Proteoglycans — The Critical Rate-Limiting Step
ApoB has positively charged regions that bind ionically to the negatively charged glycosaminoglycan chains of subendothelial proteoglycans (biglycan, versican, perlecan). This retention — not merely elevated plasma levels — is the initiating event. At early grades of atherosclerosis, lipid deposition is present in the absence of macrophages; macrophages appear later. Retention precedes and causes inflammation.
Key insight: small dense LDL binds proteoglycans more avidly than large buoyant LDL — size and density affect retention affinity independently of ApoB count
3
Modification: From Inert to Pathological
Retained LDL is exposed to secretory sphingomyelinase (S-SMase) and secretory phospholipase A2 (sPLA2), which remodel its surface and dramatically increase proteoglycan affinity — trapping particles further. Oxidizing agents convert LDL into oxidized LDL (oxLDL), which: (a) generates damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs), (b) cannot exit via LDL receptors, (c) is avidly ingested by macrophage scavenger receptors rather than the regulated LDL receptor pathway. S-SMase also aggregates particles, preventing exit through size increase alone.
4
Maladaptive Inflammatory Response
Modified retained lipoproteins trigger monocyte recruitment and differentiation into macrophages. Macrophages ingest modified LDL via scavenger receptors — forming foam cells (the hallmark of the fatty streak). Unlike receptor-mediated uptake via LDL receptors, scavenger receptor uptake is not downregulated — macrophages continue ingesting until they become lipid-engorged and dysfunctional. T-cells and mast cells follow, establishing a chronic, non-resolving inflammatory lesion.
5
Self-Amplifying Retention Loop
Macrophages release lipoprotein lipase and phospholipase-A2 into the lesion, which induce proteoglycan remodeling toward higher LDL-binding affinity — creating a self-amplifying cycle of further retention and progression. Smooth muscle cells migrate into the intima, laying down collagen to form the fibrous cap. Plaque becomes structurally defined.
6
Plaque Rupture → Clinical Event
Macrophage-secreted proteases degrade the fibrous cap, creating thin-capped, vulnerable plaque. Tissue factor exposure upon disruption triggers thrombus formation. If the thrombosed artery supplies myocardium: myocardial infarction. If it supplies the brain: ischemic stroke. The sequence from first fatty streak to clinical event spans decades of cumulative exposure.
The Inflammation Misconception
Inflammation does not independently cause atherosclerosis — it responds to retained, modified LDL. The CANTOS trial (canakinumab, anti-IL-1β) showed that targeting inflammation alone reduces CV events, which some interpret as inflammation being an independent cause. The correct interpretation: inflammation is a downstream amplifier. Without initial particle retention, the inflammatory cascade has no trigger. Reducing inflammation after particles are already retained slows progression but doesn't change the initiating mechanism.

LDL-C Causality — The Genetic Evidence Is Definitive
Ference et al., Eur Heart J 2017 · EAS Consensus Panel · >200 prospective cohort studies

The EAS Consensus Statement synthesized >200 prospective cohort studies, Mendelian randomization studies, and RCTs comprising 2+ million participants and 150,000+ CV events. The relationship is log-linear, cumulative, and consistent across every independent methodological approach. Scientists rarely use causal language; this is the exception.

Why Mendelian Randomization Establishes Causality (Not Just Association)
Ference et al., 2017 · NEJM 2016 PCSK9/HMGCR variation study

Observational epidemiology cannot prove causation — confounders and reverse causation are always possible. Mendelian randomization (MR) uses genetic variants inherited at conception as instrumental variables. Because genetic allocation is essentially random with respect to adult lifestyle factors, MR eliminates reverse causation and most confounding — analogous to randomization in a clinical trial. Key MR findings: (1) HMGCR variants (the target of statins) → same LDL-C and ASCVD risk reduction as statins themselves. (2) PCSK9 variants → same LDL-C and ASCVD reduction as PCSK9 inhibitor drugs. (3) Both genetic and pharmacological lowering produce proportionally identical risk reduction, confirming it is LDL lowering itself — not off-target drug effects — that drives benefit. The concordance of natural genetic experiments with pharmacological interventions is uniquely powerful evidence.

Critical Qualifier The EAS statement includes an essential condition: benefit requires that LDL-C reduction is "concordant with the reduction in LDL particle number." This is why evacetrapib failed — it reduced LDL-C 37% but ApoB only 15%. No clinical benefit followed (HR 1.01, p=0.91, n=12,092). Mechanisms that lower LDL-C without lowering ApoB proportionally do not reduce events. This single natural experiment validates the entire ApoB-particle-number framework.
03
ApoB Is the True Driver — What LDL-C Misses and Why It Matters
Sniderman meta-analysis · NLA Consensus 2024 · Marston et al., JAMA Cardiol 2022 · n=389,000+

LDL-C measures the mass of cholesterol inside LDL particles. ApoB measures the number of atherogenic particles (every VLDL, IDL, LDL, and Lp(a) particle carries exactly one ApoB molecule). Since atherogenesis is driven by particle retention — not cholesterol mass — particle number is the more proximate causal variable. When cholesterol per particle varies (hypertriglyceridemia, insulin resistance, sdLDL phenotype, keto diet), LDL-C and ApoB diverge, and ApoB wins every time.

The Molecular Mechanism — SREBP-2
Palmitic, myristic, and lauric acid cannot efficiently serve as substrates for the ACAT (sterol O-acyltransferase) esterification reaction in liver cells. This causes free cholesterol accumulation inside hepatocytes. The free cholesterol buildup suppresses SREBP-2 — the transcription factor that controls LDL receptor gene expression. With fewer LDL receptors on hepatocyte surfaces, LDL clearance from circulation decreases and VLDL/LDL secretion increases, raising both LDL-C and ApoB. Stearic acid (C18:0) is rapidly desaturated to oleic acid by stearoyl-CoA desaturase, bypassing this accumulation — explaining its metabolic neutrality. The DELTA feeding study confirmed this directly in humans: reducing SFA from 15% to 6% of energy increased LDL receptor abundance by 10.5% (p<0.05), with a corresponding 11.8% decrease in LDL-C (r = −0.59, p<0.01).

The Replacement Nutrient Is Everything This is the most underappreciated complexity in the SFA-CVD relationship. Meta-analyses that pool SFA reduction trials without distinguishing the replacement nutrient obscure the real finding. SFA→PUFA: CHD events reduced ~19–24% (Mozaffarian, 8 RCTs). SFA→refined carbs: no consistent benefit, potentially adverse metabolic consequences. SFA→complex carbs: modest LDL-C reduction but TC:HDL ratio unchanged. The dietary recommendation should be specific: replace C12–C16 SFAs with PUFA-rich unsaturated fats — not simply "eat less fat."
05
Clinical Trial Evidence: Strong for Statins/PCSK9i, Weaker for Dietary SFA
Cholesterol Treatment Trialists · Mozaffarian 2010 · Yamada et al. 2025 meta-analysis · Hooper et al. 2020 Cochrane

The evidence chain has different strengths at different links. The genetic and mechanistic evidence for LDL/ApoB causality is among the strongest in medicine. The direct dietary RCT evidence translating SFA reduction to hard CV event reduction is considerably weaker — not because the upstream chain is wrong, but because dietary RCTs face unique methodological challenges.

Evacetrapib is the critical natural experiment: it proves the ApoB-particle-number hypothesis in the strongest possible way. A 37% LDL-C reduction that reduced ApoB only 15% produced zero clinical benefit. Mechanisms that lower LDL-C without proportionally reducing particle count do not reduce atherosclerosis. Every successful lipid-lowering therapy reduces ApoB concordantly with LDL-C.

Why Dietary SFA RCTs Fail to Show Significance The Yamada 2025 meta-analysis (9 RCTs, n=13,532) found no significant reduction in cardiovascular mortality or MI from SFA restriction. This does not contradict the mechanistic chain. Methodological explanations: (1) Mean LDL-C reduction was only ~4 mg/dL — vs. 40+ mg/dL from high-intensity statins; (2) Nearly all trials predate statins, making background therapy non-comparable to current practice; (3) Replacement nutrients were not controlled — many used carbohydrates; (4) Trial durations of 3–9 years may be insufficient for atherosclerosis progression outcomes; (5) Sex: only 2 trials included women, neither showed benefit — leaving female-specific evidence entirely absent. The Mozaffarian PUFA-for-SFA meta-analysis (8 RCTs, PUFA specifically) does show ~19–24% CHD event reduction — suggesting the benefit is real when the replacement is correctly specified.

LDL Particle Size: Not All ApoB Particles Are Equally Atherogenic
Krauss et al. · PLoS ONE phenotype B RCT 2017 · Frontiers Cardiovasc Med 2025

ApoB particle count is the primary atherogenic driver, but particle characteristics modulate risk per particle. Small dense LDL (sdLDL) is substantially more atherogenic than large buoyant LDL on a per-particle basis — with direct implications for how SFA-induced LDL changes translate to clinical risk.

Large Buoyant LDL (Pattern A)
Lower proteoglycan affinity · less oxidation-prone
Characteristics Larger diameter (~30nm) · Higher cholesterol-to-protein ratio · Lower proteoglycan binding affinity · Shorter arterial residence time · Less susceptible to oxidative modification · More efficiently cleared by LDL receptors. SFA intake (typical ranges) predominantly raises large buoyant LDL in individuals with normal lipid phenotype (Pattern A).
Small Dense LDL (Pattern B / sdLDL)
Higher proteoglycan affinity · more atherogenic per particle
Characteristics Smaller diameter (~20nm) · Penetrates endothelium more readily · Higher proteoglycan binding affinity (binds longer, harder to clear) · Longer plasma residence time (reduced LDL receptor affinity) · Greater susceptibility to oxidation · More prone to glycation/desialylation. Elevated sdLDL associated with metabolic syndrome, insulin resistance, high TG, low HDL.
The SFA-sdLDL Nuance — Pattern B Exception
In individuals with LDL Phenotype B (preponderance of sdLDL, typically with elevated TG and insulin resistance), the response to very high SFA intake (18% of energy) is different from pattern A individuals. The PLoS ONE RCT (n=53 phenotype B individuals) found that the high SFA diet significantly increased total, medium, and small LDL particle concentrations alongside ApoB elevation — unlike the typical finding of predominantly large LDL increases in pattern A. This means: the cardiovascular impact of SFA-induced LDL-C elevation may depend substantially on baseline metabolic phenotype. Phenotype B individuals are likely at greater risk from SFA-induced changes than phenotype A individuals with equivalent LDL-C numbers.

Lipoprotein(a): The Independent Genetic Wild Card
AHA Scientific Statement 2022 · Reyes-Soffer et al. · GET-READI feeding trial 2023 · EAS Lp(a) Consensus 2022

Lp(a) is structurally similar to LDL but carries an additional protein — apolipoprotein(a) — that confers unique atherogenic and thrombotic properties through oxidized phospholipid (OxPL) binding. It is independent, causal, and for most people, immovable by lifestyle — making it a risk factor that standard lipid panels invisibly miss.

The SFA-Lp(a) Paradox — GET-READI Finding
The GET-READI RCT (n=166 African Americans) fed DASH-type diet (6% SFA) vs. average American diet (16% SFA). LDL-C fell 12 mg/dL and ApoB-100 fell 5 mg/dL — both favorable. But Lp(a) increased significantly: median 58 vs. 44 mg/dL (p<0.0001). Lp(a) is an independent, genetically determined CVD risk factor. This paradox — SFA reduction simultaneously improving and worsening atherogenic lipid markers — means the net effect of dietary SFA reduction on overall CVD risk is not uniformly positive across all individuals and populations. The mechanism driving SFA-induced Lp(a) suppression (and SFA-reduction-induced Lp(a) rise) is not established. This is a genuine unresolved complexity, particularly important for populations with disproportionately elevated Lp(a).
Atherogenicity Per Particle A 2024 JACC genetic analysis (Björnson et al.) found that Lp(a) is markedly more atherogenic than LDL on a per-ApoB-particle basis — likely due to its oxidized phospholipid (OxPL) cargo and prothrombotic properties from the apo(a) plasminogen-like domain. This means total ApoB, while superior to LDL-C, still does not fully capture atherogenic burden when Lp(a) is substantially elevated — an important limitation of any single-metric approach to lipid risk.
08
The LMHR Claim Collapsed — A Case Study in Selective Reporting
KETO-CTA retracted JACC Advances 2025 · NATURE-CT AHA 2024 · PARADIGM · ACC 2025 plaque consensus

The Lean Mass Hyper-Responder (LMHR) phenotype — LDL-C ≥200 mg/dL + HDL-C ≥80 mg/dL + TG ≤70 mg/dL on ketogenic diet — became the centerpiece of an argument that high LDL-C/ApoB is harmless in metabolically healthy individuals. The longitudinal study designed to test this claim was retracted after methodological errors too severe to correct. The actual plaque progression data told the opposite story.

⚠ RETRACTED — Soto-Mota, Norwitz, Feldman et al. · JACC Advances · 2025
"Plaque Begets Plaque, ApoB Does Not: Longitudinal Data From the KETO-CTA Trial" was retracted at the request of the authors and editors. The retraction notice states: "concerns were raised regarding the methodology in this article, which affect the reliability of the data. The authors and the Editors agree that the identified errors are too great to be corrected with a corrigendum." The paper had already drawn multiple published critiques in JACC and a Medscape commentary before retraction.
What the Authors Omitted — And What the Numbers Actually Showed
KETO-CTA trial · NCT05733325 · Authors' reply JACC Advances 2025 · NATURE-CT AHA Abstract 2024
Primary Outcome Buried The pre-registered primary endpoint was change in non-calcified coronary plaque volume (ΔNCPV). The published paper showed this only in a figure without reporting the numerical value — and emphasized PAV (percent atheroma volume), a secondary endpoint, in the text and conclusions. Multiple published letters to JACC flagged this as selective reporting. The authors only disclosed the primary outcome number on social media after public pressure.
The Hidden Number When pressed, lead author Soto-Mota disclosed on X/Twitter: median ΔNCPV = 18.8–18.9 mm³ over 1 year, corresponding to a 42.8% relative increase. The mean was 31.5 mm³ (SD 31.5 mm³) — reported in the published reply. The authors chose to report median rather than mean, which substantially minimized the apparent effect given the highly skewed distribution.
Funding & Conflicts The study was funded by Dave Feldman's own Citizen Science Foundation. Feldman is a co-author and a prominent LMHR advocate with a documented public position that high LDL-C on keto is safe. The paper's conclusion — that ApoB does not predict plaque progression in LMHRs — directly aligned with this position, while the primary outcome data suggesting accelerated plaque growth was not foregrounded.

The NATURE-CT comparison is the most damning: it was conducted in the same Budoff lab, on the same CT scanner, using the same Cleerly AI software — on a metabolically healthy population with mean LDL-C of 111 mg/dL. Their annualized median plaque progression was 4.9 mm³/yr. The LMHR group showed 18.9 mm³ in just one year — 3.86× higher than the healthy reference from the same lab. The ACC 2025 Scientific Statement defines clinically meaningful NCPV progression as ≥5–10 mm³/year. LMHRs exceeded even the upper threshold in a single year.

The Broader Methodological Failures
Published letters in JACC and Medscape identified multiple additional problems: (1) Pre-registered primary endpoint (ΔNCPV) omitted from results and conclusions — secondary endpoint (PAV) promoted instead. (2) No internal control group — the planned healthy-LDL control arm was dropped, reportedly due to funding constraints. Cross-cohort comparisons used very small subgroups (as few as n=17) without statistical testing or confounder adjustment. (3) Mean vs. median: reporting median (18.9 mm³) while the mean was 31.5 mm³ — a deliberate choice that minimized apparent effect size given the right-skewed distribution. (4) The original paper stated NCPV data was shown in Figure 1 "without proper description" — the actual value had to be extracted from social media posts. This is not how pre-registered clinical trials report their primary outcomes.
What Remains Scientifically Valid About LMHR The phenotype itself is real — lean, metabolically healthy individuals do develop dramatic LDL-C elevation on ketogenic diets through the lipid energy model mechanism (flux-driven VLDL production, not receptor impairment). The 2024 KETO Trial cross-sectional data (Budoff et al., JACC Advances 2024 — separate from the retracted longitudinal paper) showing no greater baseline plaque burden vs. matched controls at 4.7 years of exposure remains unpublished. What is gone is the claim that plaque progression is unaffected by ApoB in this population. The retracted longitudinal data, properly contextualized, showed the opposite: accelerated plaque progression at nearly 4× the healthy reference rate. The LMHR phenotype warrants clinical concern and ApoB monitoring — not reassurance.

Critical Evidence Gaps — What the Field Genuinely Does Not Know

Intellectual honesty requires explicitly flagging where the evidence chain is incomplete or where confident claims in either direction are not justified by available data.

Gap 01 · Highest Priority
No RCT has ever randomized to ApoB targets
Every landmark lipid trial used LDL-C as the primary endpoint and target. ApoB superiority evidence comes entirely from post-hoc analyses, observational studies, and natural experiments (evacetrapib). There is no prospective trial comparing "treat to ApoB target" vs. "treat to LDL-C target" with hard CV event outcomes. This is the single most important methodological gap in the ApoB literature — and the primary reason guidelines remain cautious about formally elevating ApoB to primary target status despite overwhelming epidemiological superiority.
Gap 02
SFA dietary RCTs predate statins — no statin-era data
All 9 eligible SFA reduction RCTs in the Yamada 2025 meta-analysis were conducted before widespread statin use. Whether dietary SFA reduction produces incremental CV benefit on top of high-intensity statin therapy has never been formally tested. This is the most clinically relevant question for patients already on optimized pharmacological therapy — and it is completely unanswered.
Gap 03
LDL particle size phenotype modulates SFA-risk translation
SFA predominantly raises large buoyant LDL in Phenotype A individuals, but raises all LDL subclasses including sdLDL in Phenotype B individuals. Whether SFA-induced large buoyant LDL elevation carries equivalent atherogenic risk to an equivalent ApoB elevation from sdLDL is not established. If large buoyant LDL is substantially less atherogenic per particle (lower proteoglycan affinity, slower oxidation), then Phenotype A individuals may be at lower risk from SFA-induced LDL changes than particle count alone suggests.
Gap 04
LMHR long-term hard event outcomes — decades of data don't exist
The retracted KETO-CTA longitudinal paper actually showed accelerated plaque progression (18.9 mm³/yr vs. 4.9 mm³/yr in the healthy reference population from the same lab). What does not exist is hard CV event data — MI, stroke, death — over 20–30 years. Plaque progression surrogates suggest elevated risk; whether that translates to proportionally elevated clinical events over decades in this population remains unknown. The retraction removed a false reassurance but did not replace it with hard outcome data.
Gap 05
Lp(a)-SFA interaction across populations and ancestry
The GET-READI Lp(a) elevation with SFA reduction is a single RCT in African Americans. Whether this generalizes to other populations, whether the magnitude is clinically significant given the LDL-C/ApoB benefit, and whether the net effect on overall ASCVD risk is positive or negative for high-Lp(a) individuals reducing SFA is unknown. The mechanism of SFA-modulation of Lp(a) is not established.
Gap 06
No dietary SFA RCT data in women
Only 2 of 9 eligible SFA reduction RCTs included women, and neither showed significant benefit. Whether SFA reduction differentially affects CVD risk in women vs. men — given known hormonal effects on lipoprotein metabolism, the different role of HDL-C, and distinct presentation of ASCVD in women — is entirely unknown.
Gap 07
Individual variation in ApoB response to SFA by genetics
The magnitude of LDL-C/ApoB elevation per gram of dietary SFA varies substantially across individuals, likely due to APOE genotype, LDL receptor polymorphisms, and other lipid metabolism genes. APOE4 carriers show markedly greater LDL-C responses to dietary saturated fat than APOE2 carriers. Personalized dietary fat recommendations based on lipid genetics remain aspirational but underdeveloped clinically — and no hard-outcome data by genotype exists.
Synthesis · The Complete Evidence Picture
The mechanistic chain connecting dietary SFA to ASCVD is coherent and well-supported at every link from molecular biology through genetics. Specific SFAs (C12, C14, C16) suppress hepatic SREBP-2 via intrahepatic free cholesterol accumulation → reduce LDL receptor expression → impair LDL clearance and increase VLDL/LDL secretion → elevated ApoB particle count. These particles enter the arterial wall via transcytosis, bind to subendothelial proteoglycans via ApoB's positively charged regions, undergo oxidative and enzymatic modification, and trigger the maladaptive macrophage-dominated inflammatory response that constitutes atherosclerosis. Inflammation does not start this process — it responds to it.

LDL-C causality is established by the highest quality genetic evidence: >200 cohort studies and multiple independent Mendelian randomization analyses show a log-linear, cumulative, causal relationship. ApoB is a more accurate atherogenic particle count metric than LDL-C, outperforming it in 9/9 discordance studies (593,354 participants), and in a combined analysis of 389,000+ individuals was the only lipid parameter independently associated with MI once LDL-C and TG were simultaneously modeled. The evacetrapib natural experiment is the most decisive evidence: 37% LDL-C reduction with only 15% ApoB reduction produced zero clinical benefit — proving particle count, not cholesterol mass, drives outcomes.

Where the evidence is weaker: Direct dietary RCT evidence for hard CV event reduction from SFA restriction is non-significant in meta-analyses (though trending), largely because trials used carbohydrate replacement, were pre-statin, and had insufficient LDL-C change magnitudes. The PUFA-for-SFA substitution RCTs do show ~19–24% CHD event reduction. The LMHR phenotype is real, but the primary paper claiming high ApoB does not drive plaque progression in LMHRs has been retracted for methodological errors too severe to correct. The actual plaque progression data — omitted from the published paper and only disclosed on social media after public pressure — showed LMHRs accumulated NCPV at 18.9 mm³/yr vs. 4.9 mm³/yr in a metabolically healthy reference population from the same lab with the same scanner, a 3.86× difference. This does not establish long-term hard event risk, but it definitively removes the claim that ApoB is irrelevant to plaque in this population. Lp(a) is an independent causal risk factor capturing residual risk that even ApoB-targeted therapy misses, with the paradoxical finding that SFA reduction may raise Lp(a) in certain populations.

The practical framework: Limit C12–C16 SFAs, replace with PUFA-rich unsaturated fats (not refined carbohydrates). Measure ApoB as the primary atherogenic particle burden metric. Measure Lp(a) at least once. In Phenotype B individuals, SFA-induced LDL changes carry greater per-particle atherogenic weight. The evidence supports both the dietary recommendation and the mechanistic explanation — while acknowledging that the direct RCT translation to hard outcomes is less robust than the upstream genetic and mechanistic evidence.
