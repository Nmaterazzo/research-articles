01
The 2024 JAMA Paper - Rigorous, Real, and Specifically Bounded
Loftfield et al. · JAMA Network Open · June 26 2024 · DOI: 10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2024.18729

The Loftfield 2024 study is the most rigorous observational mortality analysis of multivitamin use ever conducted. Its null result is real. It is also narrow by design - measuring longevity in healthy adults with no chronic disease using self-reported supplement use. The authors themselves carefully defined where the conclusion stops.

HR = 1.0 means identical mortality to non-users. All three CIs cross 1.0, no statistically significant effect. The slight uptick above 1.0 in adjusted models is consistent with healthy-user-effect overcorrection, not a real harm signal.
Limitation: The Healthy User Effect
MV users tend to have better diets, exercise more, and smoke less. After statistical adjustment, users may appear marginally worse because residual confounding is irreducible in observational data. The authors explicitly flagged this as a primary concern.
Limitation: Wrong Outcome for the Mechanism
Longevity was never the most plausible MV benefit pathway. Multivitamins address micronutrient gaps, not independent disease processes. Testing if a MV extends life in a nutrient-replete adult is a category error - like testing if a seatbelt improves gas mileage.
Limitation: Formulation Unmeasured
Self-reported "multivitamin use" captures whether someone took a supplement - not which formulation, dose, or baseline nutrient status. A person on a varied whole-food diet supplementing with Centrum is biologically indistinguishable from a placebo arm.
Limitation: High-Need Population Excluded
Participants with cancer or chronic disease were excluded removing the people most likely to benefit from repletion: those with absorption impairment, polypharmacy-driven depletion, or elevated metabolic demand.
What The Authors Actually Said The paper's own conclusion: "multivitamin use to improve longevity in generally healthy adults is not supported." That precision matters: generally healthy adults · longevity outcome. This says nothing about cognition, about older adults with declining absorption, or about populations with genuine nutrient deficits - exactly where the counter-evidence sits.
02
The Counter-Evidence: COSMOS and Cognition, Replicated Three Times
Baker et al. Alzheimer's & Dementia 2023 · Yeung et al. AJCN 2023 · Vyas et al. AJCN 2024

The COSMOS trial (21,442 US adults ≥60, Centrum Silver vs. placebo) confirmed no cancer or CVD benefit - consistent with Loftfield. Its cognitive substudies told a different story: three independent ancillary studies using different methodologies and non-overlapping participant samples all found significant cognitive improvement vs. placebo.

COSMOS: Effect Sizes Across Three Independent Cognitive Substudies
Cohen's d · MVM vs. placebo · 2–3 yr treatment · non-overlapping samples · Centrum Silver · adults ≥60
All three substudies reached statistical significance. No other dietary supplement has achieved triple RCT replication for cognitive outcomes in older adults. Effect sizes are modest (d ~0.10–0.14) but consistent across telephone, web-based, and in-person assessment formats.
COSMOS-Mind (Baker et al. 2023)
2,262 adults ≥65 · telephone assessments · 3 yr. Global cognition significantly higher in MVM group. Estimated cognitive aging slowed ~60% - equivalent to 1.8 years. Effect larger in participants with baseline CVD history.
COSMOS-Web (Yeung et al. 2023)
3,562 adults · internet neuropsychological testing · primary outcome: episodic memory. Significant improvement at 1 year, sustained across 3 years. Equivalent to 3.1 years of age-related memory change. Executive function: no significant effect.
COSMOS-Clinic (Vyas et al. 2024)
573 adults · in-person neuropsychological assessment - most rigorous format. Significant episodic memory benefit at 2 years. Meta-analysis across all three non-overlapping cohorts: global cognition +7%, episodic memory +6%, both statistically significant.
Why Cognition but Not Mortality?
B vitamins (B6, B12, folate) reduce homocysteine - elevated in aging adults with suboptimal B vitamin intake and an independent risk factor for cognitive decline. Many older adults have subclinical B12 insufficiency without overt deficiency symptoms. A standard MV covers this gap.
The Replication Standard Triple replication across telephone, internet, and in-person methods in a properly randomized trial is a high evidentiary bar. This is not noise. It is the strongest positive signal in the multivitamin literature - and it applies specifically to the population (older adults, higher deficiency risk) where the biological rationale is strongest.
03
The Linxian Exception: What Happens When the Gap Is Genuine
Blot et al. JNCI 1993 · Lancet 25-yr follow-up 2017 · Fan et al. Cancer 2022

The Linxian General Population Trial (1985–1991) tested four supplement combinations in 29,584 rural Chinese adults with chronic, documented micronutrient deficiencies. One arm produced the only mortality benefit ever observed in a supplement RCT in a large general population - and it endured 10 years after supplementation stopped.

Linxian: Four Supplement Arms, One Signal
29,584 adults · 2×2×2×2 factorial RCT · 5.25 yr supplementation · Blot et al. JNCI 1993
Total mortality RR
Cancer mortality RR
RR = 1.0 = no difference from placebo. Only Se+Vit E+beta-carotene reached significance. Three other arms showed no benefit. It was not "vitamins broadly" - it was one combination correcting one specific deficiency pattern in a specific population.
The Unified Interpretation Linxian and Loftfield are not contradictory - they are the same mechanism at different baseline nutritional states. A well-nourished American adult and a chronically malnourished Linxian farmer are not the same intervention target. Correcting a deficiency has consequences. Supplementing sufficiency has none. This principle, operating across populations and outcomes, explains the entire pattern of evidence.

Synthesis · How Both Verdicts Are Right
The contradiction in multivitamin research dissolves once you hold the right frame: supplements address gaps. Gaps vary by person. The outcome depends entirely on what gap, if any, exists.

The null result - no longevity benefit in well-nourished Western adults - is robust across roughly 600,000 people in multiple study designs. It is the correct answer for a generally healthy person in their 30s or 40s eating a varied diet. The mechanism makes biological sense: there is nothing to correct, so supplementing does nothing.

The cognitive signal - replicated across three independent RCT substudies in adults over 60 - is the most underreported finding in the literature. The mechanism is plausible: B12 and folate insufficiency accumulates with age due to declining absorption, and homocysteine-driven cognitive decline is a real, measurable process that a multivitamin plausibly disrupts. Mortality and cognition are different questions with different biological answers.

The Linxian data provides the proof of principle at its most extreme. A chronically malnourished population given a specific nutrient combination to correct specific deficiencies showed reduced mortality durable 10 years post-intervention. The same logic - filling a real gap has consequences - applies to the older adults in COSMOS at a smaller magnitude.

The bottom line: For most healthy adults under 60 in developed countries - no evidence of benefit for mortality or CVD. For adults over 65, particularly those with declining absorption or dietary variety - the cognitive data makes a daily standard multivitamin a reasonable, low-cost intervention with replicated RCT support.
