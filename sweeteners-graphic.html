Mehat 2022Asp/Ace-K blends vs sugar: lower energy intake across 8 RCTs, 274 participants. Lower intake could not be attributed to changes in appetite — suggesting direct caloric displacement. BG vs water: MD –0.08 mmol/L, p=0.78 (non-significant).
Miller 2014Meta-analysis of 15 RCTs + 9 cohort studies. RCTs showed consistent weight loss with LCS substitution: reduced BW, BMI, fat mass, and waist circumference vs caloric alternatives.
Key CaveatLCS vs water shows no significant body weight difference in 11 parallel-group studies (BW change +0.10 kg, 95% CI –0.87 to 1.07, Rogers 2021). The benefit is from replacing sugar — not from metabolic effects of the sweetener itself. Caloric displacement is the mechanism.
Bottom Line — Energy Intake
Replacing sugar with artificial sweeteners consistently reduces caloric intake and body weight in RCTs. The –94 kcal/day signal from 129 comparisons is robust. The claim is that they're better than actual sugar — and the evidence supports that claim.

Ahmad 2020Randomized double-blind crossover (n=17 healthy adults). Aspartame 425 mg/day + sucralose 136 mg/day for 14 days. Fecal 16S rRNA before and after. Result: no differences in dominant phyla, community structure, acetate, propionate, butyrate, isovaleric acid, valeric acid, or hexanoic acid.
SerranoHigh-dose saccharin in healthy humans and mice. No gut microbiota changes. No glucose intolerance. Directly contradicts mouse studies using supraphysiological gavage doses.
Thomson 2019Short-term sucralose consumption in healthy adults. No significant impact on metabolic response or gut microbiome composition. Published British Journal of Nutrition.
Critical ContextThe alarming gut microbiome findings come from rodent models using gavage administration at supraphysiological doses — not oral consumption at human-relevant levels. Human RCTs at realistic doses consistently show no effect.
Bottom Line — Gut Microbiome
In human RCTs using realistic doses, artificial sweeteners do not measurably alter gut microbiota composition, community structure, or short-chain fatty acid production. Animal gavage studies at 10–100× relevant doses should not be extrapolated to someone drinking a diet soda.
Synthesis · What 13 Studies Show
The controlled human evidence supports three conclusions: artificial sweeteners reduce caloric intake vs sugar (–94 kcal/day, 129 RCT comparisons), do not meaningfully affect blood glucose or insulin in healthy people (6 controlled studies, all null), and do not disrupt gut microbiome at realistic consumption doses (3 human RCTs, all null).

The important nuance: the weight benefit is specifically from replacing sugar, not from any metabolic effect of sweeteners. Sweeteners vs water is a weaker and less consistent signal. But that's the actual claim — they're a better alternative to sugar — and the evidence supports it.

The fear narrative relies on observational studies prone to reverse causation and rodent studies at supraphysiological doses. When you isolate controlled human RCTs, the picture is consistently benign at real-world consumption levels.
