Evidence Review · Protein Intake · Origin Symmetrics

The 1.62 g/kg consensus is based on a non-significant p-value. Converging evidence from IAAO studies and longitudinal re-analysis points to 2.0–2.35 g/kg for trained individuals.

Why the Morton 2018 meta-analysis breakpoint fails statistically, what IAAO methodology reveals, and where two independent lines of evidence unexpectedly agree.

1

Morton 2018's breakpoint at 1.62 g/kg has p = 0.079 (not significant) and a 95% CI of 1.03–2.20 g/kg — spanning nearly the entire experimental range.

2

Three IAAO studies in trained individuals converge on a 95% safe intake of 1.93–2.38 g/kg. The highest-quality study (Mazzulla 2020) finds 2.38 g/kg.

3

Longitudinal dose-response from 50 studies (Nunes 2022) shows positive effect size slopes through 2.4 g/kg — no hard breakpoint at 1.62 g/kg.

Simpson's Paradox — The Confounding Variable
~80% of Morton's 49 studies used untrained/novice participants who gain muscle rapidly at modest protein intakes. When high-responder novice studies are pooled with trained-athlete studies, the aggregate trend produces a spurious breakpoint. Morton's own supplementary data shows trained cohorts gaining +1.25–1.7 kg additional FFM when protein was raised from ~1.62 to 2.0–2.9 g/kg — directly contradicting the breakpoint hypothesis.
Morton's Own DataIn trained-cohort subgroups from Morton's own supplementary figures: Cribb 2007 (1.63→2.92 g/kg: +1.6 kg FFM), Hoffman 2009 (1.63→2.22 g/kg: +1.25 kg), Kerksick 2006 (1.58→2.32 g/kg: +1.7 kg), Hoffman 2006 (1.24→2.0 g/kg: +1.3 kg). All control groups consumed protein at or above the supposed breakpoint — yet further increases produced substantial gains.

Why IAAO Supersedes Nitrogen BalanceHistorical recommendations used nitrogen balance (NB) studies, which underestimate requirements by 40–50% due to unmeasured losses through skin, sweat, hair, and conceptual limitations. IAAO directly measures the point at which indicator amino acid oxidation plateaus — a direct proxy for saturation of protein synthetic capacity. No measurement error compounding.

Key Insight — No Hard Breakpoint at 1.62 g/kg
The effect size slope remains positive through 2.4 g/kg. This is graded, non-linear diminishing returns — not the sharp discontinuity implied by a "breakpoint" model. Positive effects were observed up to an average intake of 2.34 g/kg (Candow et al., 2006). The first negative effect appeared at 2.38 g/kg — in untrained participants in an extreme caloric surplus (+2000 kcal/day), which may not generalize.

Body Composition ScalingProtein requirements scale with metabolically active tissue, not total body weight. A 100 kg individual at 40% body fat (60 kg FFM) and a 100 kg individual at 10% body fat (90 kg FFM) have drastically different requirements. Use FFM scaling for precision: 2.35–2.75 g/kg FFM for hypertrophy across sexes largely eliminates the apparent male/female difference.
Meal DistributionDistribute across 4–5 meals with ~1.7–2.0g leucine minimum per meal (≈25–30g whey or 35–40g casein/beef). Spreading total daily intake matters more than maximizing leucine in any single meal. Daily total protein intake predicts ~41% of hypertrophy variance; acute post-exercise timing contributes <2% of variance independently.
Protein QualityPrioritize high-DIAAS proteins: whey isolate (~1.09), egg white (~1.08), whole egg (~1.06), milk (~1.14), beef (~0.92). Plant proteins are generally adequate when total intake hits the 2.0–2.35 g/kg target, as the lower quality is offset by volume. Blending plant sources improves amino acid completeness.
Synthesis · The Evidence Scorecard
The 1.62 g/kg recommendation rests on a non-significant p-value (0.079), a confidence interval spanning 1.03–2.20 g/kg, and a dataset dominated by untrained participants who inflate the response at low protein intakes. It was never a reliable ceiling for trained individuals.

Three IAAO studies in trained athletes converge on a 95% safe intake of 1.93–2.38 g/kg, with the most methodologically rigorous study (Mazzulla 2020) pointing to 2.38 g/kg. Fifty longitudinal studies re-analyzed by effect size slopes (Nunes 2022) show positive dose-response through 2.4 g/kg — no hard breakpoint anywhere near 1.62 g/kg.

The convergence of two independent methodologies at 2.35–2.38 g/kg provides unprecedented confidence. The practical target: 2.0–2.35 g/kg total body weight for trained males in maintenance or surplus. At 15% body fat this equals 2.35–2.75 g/kg FFM. The upper bound isn't arbitrary — it's where two independent lines of evidence converge, and where Morton's own trained-cohort data shows continued positive returns.
