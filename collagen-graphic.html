01
What Collagen Actually Is — And Why BV=0 Is the Point
Vital Proteins amino acid data · EAA biochemistry · Baar 2017 · Shaw et al., AJCN 2017

Collagen has a biological value (BV) of 0. It is an incomplete protein missing tryptophan entirely, with very low leucine. This is not a flaw — it is the most important thing to understand before evaluating collagen as a supplement. It means collagen cannot build muscle. It also means no other protein can do what collagen does.

The Rocks and Sand Analogy Think of your daily protein requirement as filling a jar. Complete proteins — whey, meat, eggs — are the rocks. They fill muscle tissue, drive myofibrillar protein synthesis, support immune function and hormones. Collagen is the sand that fills the gaps the rocks leave behind. It supplies the specific substrates — glycine, proline, hydroxyproline — that build and maintain tendons, ligaments, cartilage, skin, and bone. These are structurally separate biological functions. You cannot substitute one for the other in either direction, and counting collagen toward your daily protein quota is a category error.

The Triple Helix Structure — Why Collagen Needs Its Own Substrates
Type I collagen · Gly-X-Y repeat sequence · three intertwined α chains
Every repeat unit requires glycine in the first position — no other amino acid fits the tight core of the triple helix. This is why the body cannot synthesize collagen from generic dietary protein alone when glycine supply is limited. Modern diets largely exclude collagen-rich foods (skin, cartilage, bone broth), making glycine and hydroxyproline chronically undersupplied.

Human Plasma Data — Confirmed
A randomized double-blind crossover study in humans confirmed that between 36–47% of hydroxyproline remains in peptide-bound form in circulation after collagen hydrolysate ingestion — across bovine, porcine, and marine sources, all molecular weights. The bioactive di/tripeptides (Pro-Hyp, Hyp-Gly) peak in human plasma at 60–120 minutes and directly stimulate fibroblast collagen production. This is human data, not animal.

Source Doesn't Matter Much — Once Hydrolyzed
Marine, bovine, and porcine hydrolyzed collagen show comparable plasma Hyp kinetics when matched for molecular weight. The "marine collagen is more bioavailable" claim is primarily a molecular weight argument — marine sources are typically processed to ~2,000 Da vs. bovine at 3,000–8,000 Da. When equalized, absorption rates are comparable across species.
On the Isotopic Tracer Evidence
The most compelling mechanistic evidence — that collagen peptides specifically deposit into collagenous tissues (cartilage, skin) at 2× the concentration of free proline controls — comes from radioactively labeled tracer studies in mice. This direct tissue-deposition proof cannot ethically be replicated in humans. What exists in humans: confirmed plasma absorption of intact bioactive peptides, elevated PINP collagen synthesis markers (Shaw 2017, human), measurable increases in dermal collagen density by ultrasound (Asserin 2015, human), and fibroblast stimulation in human cell culture. The mechanistic chain is plausible and partially confirmed in humans — the specific "preferential cartilage deposition" claim relies on animal tracers not yet directly replicated in people.

Collagen Types — What Actually Matters for Supplementation
Meaghers Pharmacy type review · Paleo Pro 2026 · UC-II clinical data

There are 28 identified collagen types. Three account for 80–90% of the body's total. For supplementation, the type distinction only matters in one specific scenario — and "5 types in one scoop" products are almost entirely marketing.

TYPE I
Skin · Tendons · Ligaments · Bone · Cornea
90% of total body collagen. Dense rope-like triple helix, extremely high tensile strength. The primary type in bovine hide and marine sources. Most clinical evidence for skin, joint, and tendon/ligament outcomes. This is what "hydrolyzed collagen peptides" almost always means.
TYPE II
Articular Cartilage (60–95% of cartilage collagen)
Resists compression rather than tension. Two distinct supplementation mechanisms: Hydrolyzed Type II (10g+) provides cartilage-building substrates. Undenatured Type II / UC-II (40mg — tiny dose) works via oral immune tolerance — intact cartilage antigen triggers regulatory T cells that suppress autoimmune cartilage attack. UC-II outperformed glucosamine + chondroitin in clinical trials and works through a completely different pathway than hydrolyzed collagen.
TYPE III
Skin · Blood Vessels · Intestinal Walls · Muscles (ECM)
Second most abundant (5–20% of body collagen). Always found alongside Type I. Flexible mesh structure — laid down first during tissue formation, then replaced by Type I as tissue matures. This is why younger skin and connective tissue are more supple. Bovine hide provides both Types I and III.
V & X
Marketing Only — No Human Supplementation Data
Type V (cornea, hair) and Type X (bone growth plates) appear in "5-type collagen" blend products. Neither has been studied as a standalone supplement in human trials. Their inclusion is pure marketing differentiation with zero evidence base.
Why Type Specificity Is Mostly Irrelevant for Hydrolyzed Forms Once collagen is hydrolyzed, the bioactive di/tripeptides (Pro-Hyp, Hyp-Gly) are similar regardless of which type they came from. The body uses these substrates to synthesize whichever collagen type the target tissue requires. Type-specificity matters primarily for undenatured Type II (UC-II) — because its mechanism depends on the intact antigen structure, not the amino acids. For everything else: get hydrolyzed collagen peptides (predominantly Type I ± III from bovine or marine), don't overthink the source.

Why Vitamin C and Timing Both Matter Collagen synthesis requires two enzymatic hydroxylation steps — proline → hydroxyproline and lysine → hydroxylysine — both catalyzed by vitamin C-dependent enzymes. Without adequate vitamin C, the collagen cross-linking process is incomplete. Timing matters because amino acids from collagen peak in blood at 60 minutes post-ingestion. Exercise provides the mechanical loading signal. The combination — substrate availability + mechanical stimulus — is what drives the synergistic PINP response. Neither alone produces the same effect.
Skin Hydration & Elasticity — The Industry Funding Problem
Myung & Park, Am J Med 2025 (23 RCTs, n=1,474) · Pu et al. 2023 (26 RCTs, n=1,721)
Pooled Analysis — Positive
A meta-analysis of 26 RCTs (1,721 patients) found hydrolyzed collagen significantly improved skin hydration (p<0.00001) and elasticity (p<0.00001) vs. placebo. A second meta-analysis of 23 RCTs (1,474 patients) confirmed significant improvements in hydration, elasticity, and wrinkles across all studies.
Stratified by Funding — Evidence Collapses
Myung & Park (2025) — the only meta-analysis to separate studies by funding source — found that studies not funded by pharmaceutical companies showed no effect on skin hydration, elasticity, or wrinkles. Industry-funded studies showed significant effects. High-quality studies showed no significant effect; low-quality studies showed improvement. The mechanistic case is real (Pro-Hyp directly stimulates fibroblasts and hyaluronic acid synthesis in human cells). The independent clinical evidence is not yet there.

What Collagen Does Nothing For — Skeletal Muscle
Oikawa et al. 2020 · Holm et al. 2017 · Stuart Phillips protein research group

This is the clearest part of the evidence base and the most frequently misrepresented. Collagen supplementation does not build muscle, does not drive strength gains, and should never be counted toward a daily protein target used for muscle goals.

The Mechanism — Why This Is Inevitable
Myofibrillar protein synthesis (building actin and myosin — contractile muscle proteins) requires leucine to trigger mTOR signaling, and all nine essential amino acids present for translation elongation to complete. If even one EAA is missing, the ribosome stalls — the protein cannot be synthesized. Collagen has zero tryptophan and ~3% leucine. This is not a dose problem. No amount of collagen can trigger MPS because the structural requirement cannot be met. Strength gains are tied to myofibrillar synthesis. Collagen does not stimulate it.
What Collagen Does Do Within Muscle — The ECM
Collagen does support the extracellular matrix (ECM) — the collagenous scaffolding surrounding muscle fibers (epimysium, perimysium, endomysium). This is structurally continuous with tendons and ligaments. Some studies show improved body composition with collagen + resistance training — but biopsy data shows no change in fiber cross-sectional area (fCSA), meaning no myofibrillar hypertrophy. The composition change is passive connective tissue adaptation, not muscle growth. These are different tissues doing different jobs.

Critical: Do Not Count Collagen Toward Your Protein Target Collagen's BV for muscle is 0. If your daily protein goal is 160g and you take 15g of collagen, your effective protein for muscle purposes is still 160g — the collagen operates on a completely separate substrate pathway. Taking collagen does not allow you to reduce complete protein intake. They are additive, not substitutable. Think of it as a structural supplement in the same category as creatine — not a protein source.

Synthesis · The Complete Picture
Hydrolyzed collagen is one of the more genuinely useful supplements in the market — not because of inflated claims, but because it does something specific that almost nothing else does: it supplies the glycine, proline, and hydroxyproline substrates that modern diets chronically undersupply, and it delivers them in a bioavailable form that directly reaches collagenous tissues.

What is well-established: Joint pain reduction (35 RCTs, moderate-to-high certainty, outperforms glucosamine and chondroitin). Connective tissue collagen synthesis augmentation with the Shaw protocol (15g + vitamin C, 60 min pre-exercise — human PINP data). Superior bioavailability of hydrolyzed forms over gelatin and bone broth. The type distinction is mostly irrelevant once hydrolyzed — get Type I/III from bovine or marine and don't overthink it.

What is mechanistically sound but clinically contested: Skin hydration and elasticity — the fibroblast stimulation pathway is real and confirmed in human cells, but clinical evidence collapses when funding source is controlled. Independent replication is needed.

What it definitively cannot do: Build muscle, drive myofibrillar protein synthesis, or substitute for complete protein. The BV=0 for muscle is not a dosing problem — it's a structural amino acid profile problem that no amount of collagen can overcome. Count it as a structural supplement, not a protein source.

Practical approach: 5–10g/day hydrolyzed collagen peptides for general joint and connective tissue support. 15g + 50mg vitamin C, 60 minutes before exercise sessions that load the target tissue, for active tendon/ligament remodeling. Add it to your morning coffee, a smoothie, or pre-workout — it's tasteless, dissolves cold, and the evidence for joint outcomes is among the strongest in the supplement space.
