The WHO's 18% increased risk from processed meat is real — but it's relative, not absolute. That distinction changes everything.

What the headlines imply vs what the data actually shows — with the exact numbers from the papers.

What People Hear
Processed meat raises cancer risk 18%
Most people interpret this as: 4% becomes 22%. An 18 percentage-point jump. That is not what the study found.
What the Data Shows
4.3% becomes 5.07%
HR = 1.18 is a proportional multiplier. 4.3% × 1.18 = 5.07%. Absolute increase: 0.77 percentage points.
01
The WHO Study — What It Found
Chan et al., 2011 · PLOS ONE · 28 studies
The PaperChan DSM et al. (2011). Red and Processed Meat and Colorectal Cancer Incidence: Meta-Analysis of Prospective Studies. PLoS ONE. 42 articles, 28 prospective studies, 11,358 CRC cases. The primary meta-analysis behind the WHO IARC Group 1 classification of processed meat.
Key ResultProcessed meat: RR = 1.18 per 50g/day (95% CI 1.10–1.28). Combined red + processed: RR = 1.14 per 100g/day (95% CI 1.04–1.24). These are relative risk multipliers — not percentage-point increases.
HR = 1.18 ExplainedA hazard ratio of 1.18 is an 18% proportional increase over your baseline — not 18 percentage points added. Baseline lifetime CRC risk for men is 4.3%. Apply HR 1.18: 4.3% × 1.18 = 5.07%. Absolute difference: 0.77%. For comparison, smoking raises lung cancer risk with an HR of ~20 in heavy smokers. Group 1 classification describes evidence quality, not magnitude of harm.

Why This Matters
A minimum HR of 2.0–4.0 is generally considered necessary to suggest meaningful causal magnitude in epidemiology. HR = 1.18 is statistically significant — but small in effect size. The same Group 1 classification covers tobacco (HR ~20 for lung cancer) and processed meat. Classification describes evidence quality, not dose of harm.

Key ObservationHigh processed meat + high vegetables (HR 1.66) has nearly the same risk as low processed meat + low vegetables (HR 1.56). The vegetable signal is stronger and more consistent than the meat signal.
Vegetable EffectAcross all combinations, the average cancer risk reduction between high vs low vegetable/fruit groups was approximately ~46%. That dwarfs the 18% relative increase from processed meat.
Missing ConfounderThe Chan 2011 meta-analysis does not consistently control for vegetable, fruit, and fiber intake. People who eat more processed meat tend to eat fewer vegetables — making it difficult to isolate meat from the overall dietary pattern.
The Real Takeaway
High vegetables + high meat (HR 1.66) nearly matches low vegetables alone (HR 1.56). Vegetables are the dominant protective variable. This is why every longitudinal study controlling for vegetable intake shows greater risk in low-vegetable consumers — the meat and vegetable stories are inseparable.
Synthesis · What Education Looks Like
The WHO Group 1 classification for processed meat is based on real evidence — consistent across 28 prospective studies. But HR 1.18 per 50g/day translates to a 0.77 percentage-point absolute increase in lifetime colorectal cancer risk — not 18 percentage points. 4.3% becoming 5.07%.

More importantly: the vegetable and fruit signal is larger and more consistent. In 26,218 adults followed 13.5 years, high vegetables offset most of the meat risk (HR 1.66 with high meat + vegetables vs HR 1.56 with low meat and no vegetables). Diet quality as a whole drives cancer risk — not individual foods in isolation.
