01
J-Curve: 30 Years of a Statistical Artifact
Zhao et al. JAMA Network Open 2023 · Stockwell et al. J Stud Alcohol Drugs 2016 · 107 cohort studies · 4,838,825 participants

The J-shaped risk curve suggested moderate drinkers had lower all-cause mortality than abstainers, with risk rising at heavy intake. This became embedded in public health messaging for decades. A 2023 meta-analysis of 107 cohort studies containing 4.8 million participants showed what happens when you actually correct for the biases baked into those prior studies.

The J-Curve: What Studies Found vs. What Was Actually There
Unadjusted vs. bias-corrected relative risk of all-cause mortality by daily alcohol intake · Zhao et al. JAMA Network Open 2023
Unadjusted (shows J-curve)
Bias-corrected (lifetime abstainers as reference)
When former drinkers and sick quitters are removed from the abstainer reference group and study quality covariates are controlled for, the J-curve disappears. The apparent protective dip at low doses was a methodological artifact, not a biological effect.
What the bad studies did
Mixed former drinkers into the "abstainer" group. People who quit drinking often quit because they got sick. This makes abstainers look artificially unhealthy.
Used older cohorts. In older populations, the abstainer group fills up with sick quitters over time, inflating the apparent benefit of moderate drinking.
Compared drinkers to current abstainers, not lifetime abstainers. Occasional drinkers who rarely drink were counted as "abstainers," again polluting the reference group.
What the corrected analysis found
86 of 107 studies included former or occasional drinkers in the abstainer reference group. Only 21 were fully clean.
In younger cohorts (enrolled under 50, less contaminated by sick quitters), the J-curve protection disappeared entirely and risk was monotonically linear.
Once corrected, low-volume drinking showed no significant reduction in all-cause mortality. The benefit was statistical noise created by a biased comparison group.
What "Moderate" Actually Means in Grams In the fully adjusted models, drinking below 25g/day in women and below 45g/day in men showed no statistically significant mortality increase. One US standard drink is 14g of ethanol. So the women's threshold is roughly 1.5 drinks per day and the men's threshold is roughly 3 drinks per day. Occasional drinking of a few drinks per week sits comfortably below either threshold from a mortality standpoint. For cancer, there is no floor at any dose, but the absolute risk increase at very low consumption is genuinely small and not meaningfully detectable in any real study.
02
Mendelian Randomization: The Closest Thing to an RCT We Have
Holmes et al. BMJ 2014 · Kasseh et al. IJE 2024 · Million Veteran Program MR study · 261,991 individuals

Mendelian randomization uses genetic variants that naturally predict how much someone drinks as a proxy for a randomized experiment. People who carry certain variants of the ADH1B gene metabolize alcohol faster, find it less pleasant, and drink less as a result. This is not a choice they made, so it avoids the lifestyle confounding that plagues observational studies. The results across multiple large MR studies are consistent.

Significant harm (* p<0.05)
Mendelian randomization consistently finds no protective cardiovascular effect and shows linear harm for mortality and stroke. The pattern is the opposite of what observational studies suggested.
Why MR is more reliable
Genetic variants are assigned at conception, before anyone starts drinking. They cannot be confounded by diet, SES, or lifestyle choices that make moderate drinkers healthier in observational studies. The ADH1B gene variant that makes people drink less is a natural randomization experiment across millions of people.
What MR cannot rule out
MR assumes the genetic variant only affects outcomes through alcohol (no pleiotropy). ADH1B may have other effects. MR also compresses effects toward the mean, so it is best interpreted alongside, not instead of, well-designed observational data. But the direction is clear: no cardioprotection, and some cardiovascular harm.
03
Cancer: The Evidence That Does Not Have a Threshold
IARC Group 1 classification 1988, updated 2007 2009 · NCI Alcohol Fact Sheet · Gapstur et al. NEJM 2023 · 741,300 cancer cases/year globally

Unlike the mortality question, the alcohol-cancer relationship is not particularly contested. IARC has classified alcoholic beverages as a Group 1 carcinogen since 1988, with updates adding breast and colorectal cancer in 2007 and 2009. The mechanism is well understood. The dose-response is linear. There is no protective floor.

Alcohol-Attributable Cancer Burden by Type (2020 Global Estimates)
IARC Evidence Summary Brief No.6 2025 · 741,300 total alcohol-attributable cancer cases in 2020
Breast cancer is particularly important: risk is elevated even at one drink per day in women. Most people are aware of the liver cancer link. Fewer than 20% of people asked about breast cancer name alcohol as a cause.
How Alcohol Causes Cancer: Three Confirmed Mechanisms
IARC Handbooks Volume 20A 2024 · NCI Alcohol and Cancer Fact Sheet · Gapstur et al. NEJM 2023
All three mechanisms are biologically reversible upon cessation. The IARC evidence review found strong evidence that acetaldehyde damage, DNA alterations, and gut permeability all decrease within months to years after stopping drinking.
The Breast Cancer Finding Deserves Its Own Callout Women who have just one drink per day have a measurably higher risk of breast cancer than those who drink less than one per week. Awareness of this is shockingly low: only 10-20% of people identify alcohol as a cause of breast cancer when asked, compared to 40% who correctly identify the liver cancer link. This is not a heavy-drinking problem. It is a light-drinking problem.
04
Shao et al. 2024 (UK Biobank): A Study with a Useful Finding and a Big Caveat
Shao et al. Medicine 2024 · 502,490 UK Biobank participants · 11.9 year follow-up · prospective cohort design

This is a large, well-powered study with a useful finding buried inside it. It found a U-shaped relationship between wine, beer, and champagne and CVD risk, with a protective signal below about 10-11 g/day. It also found that spirits showed no U-curve at all, only monotonically rising harm. Here is why you were right to be skeptical.

The spirits finding is the most honest data
Spirits drinkers are demographically different from wine drinkers. Wine drinkers in the UK Biobank skew toward higher income, better diet, and more Mediterranean-adjacent lifestyles. Spirits drinkers skew the opposite way. The fact that spirits showed no J-curve at all, while wine and champagne did, suggests the protective signal tracks the person drinking, not the drink.
Same design flaw Zhao corrected for
The Shao study is an observational cohort and does not appear to use lifetime abstainers as the reference group. This is exactly the drinker misclassification error that inflated the J-curve in the studies Zhao 2023 corrected. The U-shaped CVD finding for wine is almost certainly the healthy user effect, not a biological signal from alcohol itself.
Cancer finding is the most useful part
The paper found that none of the beverage types, at any dose, showed a protective effect on cancer risk. Even within a study that found a CVD J-curve, the cancer dose-response was entirely harmful with no protective floor. This is consistent with the IARC consensus.
Safe dose thresholds it quotes are not validated
The paper concludes that less than 11 g/day for men and less than 10 g/day for women is a "safe dose." These numbers come from the threshold below which the U-curve dips. Given the methodological limitations, you should not interpret these as meaningful clinical thresholds. They are descriptive statistical artifacts from a biased analysis.
05
Wine's Polyphenols Are Not Worth the Alcohol
Linus Pauling Institute resveratrol review · Drago et al. Food Frontiers 2024 · Bioavailability studies in 25 humans (Guerrero et al.)

Red wine's apparent cardiovascular benefits were long attributed to resveratrol, a polyphenol in grape skins. This story has not survived rigorous testing. The dose problem alone dismantles it, before you even get to bioavailability.

The Resveratrol Dose Gap: What Wine Delivers vs. What Studies Test
Linus Pauling Institute · Drago et al. Food Frontiers 2024 · clinical trial dose ranges for cardiovascular and cancer outcomes
A glass of wine delivers 0.3 to 0.5 mg of resveratrol. Clinical trials testing cardiovascular effects use at least 8-100 mg. Anti-aging and cognitive trials use 250-2000 mg. The dose from wine is 100 to 4000 times below what any study tests. Even at those supplement doses, bioavailability is low because resveratrol is rapidly conjugated in the gut and liver before it can act systemically.
What wine actually contains
Red wine does contain polyphenols beyond resveratrol, including quercetin, anthocyanins, and catechins, with genuine antioxidant activity. The problem is not that these compounds are inactive. The problem is that you deliver them alongside ethanol and acetaldehyde, both Group 1 carcinogens, at concentrations far below what any clinical study has shown to be effective. You can get the same polyphenols from grapes, berries, or dark chocolate without the carcinogens.
The French Paradox revisited
The French Paradox (high saturated fat, high wine, low CVD) that launched the resveratrol hypothesis is now largely explained by dietary confounding, the lag effect (French CVD deaths rose as American-style diets spread), and the same drinker misclassification errors that plagued the J-curve literature. It was a hypothesis built on observational correlation, not a validated biological mechanism.

Synthesis: The Bottom Line
The 30-year story of "moderate drinking is good for you" has collapsed under better methodology. The J-curve was real in the data. It was not real in the biology. When you use lifetime abstainers as the comparison group and remove former drinkers who quit because they got sick, the apparent protective dip at low doses disappears. Mendelian randomization, which gets around confounding entirely by using genetic variants as a natural experiment, confirms: no cardiovascular protection, and some linear harm for stroke and all-cause mortality.

Cancer is a separate and clearer story. Alcohol causes at least 7 types of cancer through three well-understood mechanisms. There is no safe threshold. The dose-response is linear from the first drink. This is not particularly contested. The breast cancer finding deserves special attention because it applies to very low doses and is almost completely absent from public awareness.

The wine argument does not hold up. Resveratrol at the dose in a glass of wine is meaninglessly small compared to what clinical trials test. The polyphenols are real, but you can get them from grapes, berries, or dark chocolate without the carcinogens. The apparent health advantage of wine drinkers versus spirit drinkers in observational studies reflects who is drinking wine, not what is in the wine.

What this means practically: Occasional drinking at low frequency sits so far below the mortality thresholds that the risk is genuinely small. But small is not zero, particularly for cancer. If you drink rarely and enjoy it, the honest answer from the evidence is that the risk is low enough to be a lifestyle choice rather than a health decision. If you are a woman drinking one drink per day and thought you were in a safe zone based on old guidelines, the current evidence says you should know about the breast cancer signal. And if someone tells you that red wine is good for your heart, they are repeating a story the data stopped supporting years ago.
