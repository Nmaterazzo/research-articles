All-Cause Mortality — The Neutral Story
Meta-Analyses · Cohort Studies
RR per 200g/day
0.99–1.01
Total dairy — all-cause mortality. Statistically indistinguishable from no effect.
I² Heterogeneity
94%
For non-fermented milk & all-cause mortality. So high that pooled HR estimates are unreliable.
Dose Response
None
Incremental increases in dairy consumption don't alter mortality risk in either direction.
Consistent Overviews of systematic reviews and meta-analyses show milk, cheese, yogurt, and butter all report RR of 0.96–1.01 per 200g/day — none significantly different from null. Total dairy consumption does not alter overall longevity in either direction within typical consumption ranges.
Caveat Specific cause-of-death data is murkier. CVD associations (I²=93%), cancer (I²=75%) show substantial variability — suggesting the effect may differ by population, geography, or dairy subtype even if the aggregate signal is flat.
Context The famous Michaëlsson Swedish cohort (≥3 glasses/day → HR 1.93 in women) is a single observational study. When synthesized into meta-analyses with other cohorts, this signal disappears into heterogeneity rather than driving a consistent pooled effect.
Bottom Line — Mortality
Milk is not killing you and it's not saving you from dying. The highest-level evidence says null effect on all-cause mortality. The inconsistencies in individual studies are absorbed into noise at the meta-analytic level, not resolved into a consistent signal.

Milk Intake & Hip Fracture Risk — The Non-Linear Dose-Response
Meta-analysis of prospective cohort studies · Milk consumption & fragility fractures
Paradox Risk of hip fracture rises as milk consumption increases from 0 → 600 g/day in cohort meta-analyses — then plateaus. 200 g/day may have a modest beneficial threshold. Higher is not better, and may be worse.
Fermented Wins Yogurt shows consistent 22% lower hip fracture risk (RR 0.78) across meta-analyses. Fermentation alters gut microflora, provides anti-inflammatory effects, and changes the nutrient matrix in ways plain milk does not.
Geography Matters Milk shows reduced fracture risk in US populations (RR 0.75) but not Scandinavian (RR 1.00). Hypothesis: vitamin D fortification of milk in the US, not the milk itself, drives the benefit.
No RCTs Zero large-scale randomized controlled trials directly measuring fracture incidence with milk intervention exist. All fracture data is observational — subject to residual confounding, recall bias, and reverse causation.
Bottom Line — Fractures
The "milk = strong bones = no fractures" equation doesn't hold at the evidence level. Yogurt and cheese show more consistent protection than milk. The dose-response for milk is non-linear — and ecological data shows countries with the highest milk intake have the highest fracture rates. Vitamin D fortification may be doing the work that's being attributed to milk itself.

RCT Gold Standard This is the only outcome with meta-analyses of RCTs as the evidence base — the highest level of causal inference. BMD improvements are small but consistent across children, adults, and postmenopausal women at multiple skeletal sites.
Context-Dependent Benefits are significantly more pronounced in individuals with baseline calcium intake below 700 mg/day and in longer trials (>1 year). If you're already meeting calcium targets from diverse sources, the marginal benefit of additional milk on BMD may be negligible.
Mechanism Known Calcium + protein → suppress PTH → reduce bone resorption. Protein in milk also directly stimulates intestinal calcium absorption. The biological pathway is well-characterized — this isn't correlation, it's understood mechanism.
BMD ≠ Fracture Prevention Higher BMD from RCTs does not translate into demonstrated fracture reduction in observational data. Bone quality, microarchitecture, fall risk, and physical activity all determine fracture — BMD is one input, not the whole picture.
Bottom Line — BMD
This is where milk earns its reputation — but with a crucial caveat. RCT data consistently shows BMD improvements, particularly in calcium-deficient populations. The mechanism is understood. But BMD is not the same as fracture prevention, and the two outcome literatures tell completely different stories despite everyone assuming they're the same question.
Synthesis · The Evidence Scorecard
Milk is neutral on mortality, complicated on fractures, and modestly effective for BMD — and these three findings do not all point in the same direction, which is exactly why "milk is good for your bones" is an oversimplification.

The most important reframe: yogurt and cheese outperform milk specifically on fracture data, vitamin D fortification may explain much of the geographic benefit attributed to milk, and BMD improvements are real but context-dependent — they're most meaningful when calcium intake is suboptimal. More milk for someone already hitting calcium targets from varied whole foods is doing very little incremental work.

The public health message should be: fermented dairy for fracture protection, calcium-adequate intake via whatever source fits your diet, moderate milk as part of a mixed approach, and no evidence that higher milk intake beyond moderate amounts provides additional benefit — and some observational signals suggest the opposite.
