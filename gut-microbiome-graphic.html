01
The Field Cannot Define What It Is Trying to Optimize
Wastyk et al. Cell 2021 · Dahl et al. British Journal of Nutrition 2020 · Nature Microbiology "Hype to Hope" editorial 2024 · PubMed search June 2024

Before you can improve something, you need to know what the good version looks like. The gut microbiome field has a foundational problem: despite tens of thousands of papers and global sequencing efforts, researchers have not established a universal definition of a healthy microbiome. Without that baseline, every claim about optimizing your gut is built on an undefined target.

More papers does not mean more answers. Publication volume has exploded while the foundational question, what does a healthy microbiome actually look like, remains unanswered. This gap is what the supplement industry lives in.
The British Journal of Nutrition verdict
Despite tens of thousands of papers and huge efforts in characterising the gut microbiota of humans across the planet, science is still not able to define a "healthy microbiome" or to give specific recommendations on how to alter the microbiome to affect health outcomes. This was published in 2020 and remains accurate in 2025.
The individual variability problem
The Human Microbiome Project found that each person carries around 600,000 microbial genes in the gut, roughly half shared between individuals. But the other half is highly individual. A single strain per species typically dominates each person's microbiome and preserves its own stability over time, making universal dietary recommendations almost impossible to validate.
What "healthy" depends on
Defining who counts as a "healthy" reference subject is itself contested. Is it someone with no current illness? An athlete at peak performance? An elderly person who has lived to 90? Centenarians? Each answer gives you a different microbiome profile. You cannot define a healthy microbiome without first defining health, and that definition is not settled.
Transit time: the overlooked confounder
Gut transit time, how fast food moves through your colon, is one of the strongest predictors of microbiome composition. It varies dramatically between people and is almost never controlled for in dietary studies. Two people eating the identical diet can have radically different microbiomes just because of transit time differences, which collapses the idea that specific foods reliably produce specific microbial outcomes.
02
"Dysbiosis" Is a Marketing Word, Not a Clinical Diagnosis
Walker and Hoyles, Nature Microbiology 2023 · Joos et al. Nature Reviews Microbiology 2024 · Lancet Gastroenterology consensus 2025

Walk into any supplement store or visit any gut health brand's website and you will see "dysbiosis" used confidently, implying a diagnosable condition that their product corrects. The problem is that the leading microbiome researchers in the world have published consensus papers explicitly calling the term vague, circular, and clinically meaningless.

The Dysbiosis Circular Definition Problem
Walker and Hoyles, Nature Microbiology 2023 · Hooks and O'Malley 2017 · PMC literature review
When researchers traced the definition of dysbiosis through the literature, they found it refers to homeostasis, which is itself rarely defined. This circular logic has "led to a catchall definition lacking scientific value" according to published analysis of the field.
What the 2025 Lancet consensus actually said
An international expert panel published consensus guidelines in the Lancet Gastroenterology on microbiome testing. On the Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes ratio, the most commonly reported metric in consumer gut tests, the panel was explicit: it should be excluded from clinical reporting because there is insufficient evidence to establish a causal relationship between this ratio and host health.
Post-testing advice is "strongly discouraged"
The same 2025 consensus statement noted that direct-to-consumer tests proliferating without regulatory oversight result in "considerable waste of patient and health-care resources due to inappropriate requests for medical exams or inappropriate subsequent prescribing of supplements and medications." Post-testing therapeutic advice by the testing provider is strongly discouraged. That is the panel's language, not a summary.
Microbes are not inherently good or bad
Walker and Hoyles in Nature Microbiology 2023: "Microorganisms and their metabolites are neither 'good' nor 'bad', they merely exist." The popular framing of certain bacteria as villains and others as heroes does not reflect how the ecology actually works. Healthy individuals carry Clostridioides difficile in their gut throughout life. It only causes problems in specific contexts. Context is everything. Binary good/bad framing is not science.
The DNA nutrition parallel
The gut microbiome testing industry is following the same playbook as DNA-based personalized nutrition: take a real but early-stage scientific signal, translate it into a consumer product well before the evidence supports actionable recommendations, and market it as precision health. DNA-based diet matching has not been validated in RCTs. Microbiome-based diet matching is not there yet either.
The Chicken or the Egg Problem Even when a microbiome difference is found between sick and healthy people, the field cannot consistently determine which came first. Did the altered microbiome contribute to the disease, or did the disease alter the microbiome? This causation question is almost never resolved in observational studies, and most microbiome research is observational. Without knowing the direction of causation, you cannot build a dietary intervention.
03
Fackelmann et al. (Nature Microbiology 2025): The Biggest Diet-Microbiome Study to Date
Fackelmann et al. Nature Microbiology 2025 · 21,561 individuals · 5 multinational cohorts · vegan, vegetarian, omnivore diet patterns

It is genuinely large and well-designed relative to most microbiome research. It found real microbiome differences between vegans, vegetarians, and omnivores, with certain omnivore-associated bacteria negatively correlated with cardiometabolic markers. The conclusion many people drew from it is bigger than what the study can actually support.

Diet Pattern vs. Gut Microbiome Signatures: Key Bacteria and Their Associations
Fackelmann et al. Nature Microbiology 2025 · AUC = 0.85 for classifying diet from microbiome profile alone
The study found an AUC of 0.85, meaning gut bacteria alone could classify diet pattern in 85% of cases. This is a strong signal that diet shapes the microbiome. It does not tell us which specific foods drive which specific bacteria, or whether those bacteria causally produce the cardiometabolic associations shown here.
The Correct Takeaway The study essentially confirms: eating more fruits and vegetables is associated with favorable gut bacteria, which are associated with favorable cardiometabolic markers. That directional finding is real. But it is built on self-reported dietary data, and the associations between those bacteria and health outcomes are correlational, not causal. The paper cannot tell you to eat a specific food to produce a specific bacterial shift to produce a specific health outcome. That chain of causation has not been established.
04
TMAO: The Cardiovascular Villain That Mendelian Randomization Could Not Find
Canyelles et al. IJMS 2023 · Jia et al. Diabetes 2019 · TMAO/ischemic stroke MR meta-analysis 2023 · secondary RCT analysis 2024

Trimethylamine N-oxide became one of the most-cited arguments for why red meat is uniquely harmful: gut bacteria convert choline from animal foods into TMAO, which causes cardiovascular disease. This story went viral. The Mendelian randomization evidence has consistently failed to support the causal part of that claim.

The TMAO Pathway and the Fish Paradox
Canyelles et al. IJMS 2023 · secondary RCT analysis (omega-3/whole grain diets raise TMAO but lower CVD risk) · MR studies 2019-2023
When researchers controlled for renal function in prospective studies, the association between TMAO and CVD outcomes was significantly reduced or reversed. Mendelian randomization studies, which bypass confounding, generally found no causal relationship between genetically predicted TMAO and cardiometabolic disease.
What MR actually found
Three Mendelian randomization studies on TMAO have been published. One found no significant association between genetically predicted TMAO and cardiometabolic disease. Another found a positive relationship with systolic blood pressure that largely disappeared in sensitivity analyses. No MR study has confirmed a causal link between TMAO and CVD events, cardiac mortality, or stroke.
The renal confound
The association between high TMAO and CVD tends to disappear or reverse once kidney function is adjusted for in prospective studies. TMAO is eliminated by the kidneys. People with kidney disease accumulate TMAO and also have very high cardiovascular risk. The association may be measuring kidney dysfunction masquerading as a gut-microbiome effect.
05
What the Evidence Actually Supports
Sonnenburg et al. Cell 2021 · Cochrane FMT review 2023 · Walker and Hoyles Nature Microbiology 2023 · systematic review of probiotic RCTs

The microbiome is genuinely important to health. The science just has not advanced far enough to turn that importance into specific dietary prescriptions. Here is an honest accounting of what has solid evidence, what has directional signals, and what is speculation.

Evidence Quality by Intervention: What the Data Actually Supports
Cochrane reviews · Sonnenburg et al. Cell 2021 · systematic reviews 2022-2024 · Nature Microbiology editorial 2024
FMT: The one clear win
Fecal microbiota transplantation for recurrent C. difficile infection has multiple RCTs and Cochrane review support. It is the gold standard evidence for microbiome intervention causally affecting a clinical outcome. It is also the most technically invasive intervention possible and not remotely comparable to taking a probiotic supplement.
The Sonnenburg 2021 RCT
This Stanford RCT (n=36) found that a high-fermented food diet increased microbial diversity and decreased 19 inflammatory markers including IL-6, IL-12p70, and IFN-gamma compared to a high-fiber diet over 10 weeks. It is the most controlled dietary intervention study on microbiome outcomes. It is small, and fermented food diversity (yogurt, kefir, kimchi, kombucha) is the specific intervention, not a probiotic pill.
The Antibiotic Finding Is the Strongest Causal Evidence in the Field A single course of broad-spectrum antibiotics can reduce gut microbial diversity for six months or longer. This is the clearest demonstration that the microbiome is causally alterable and that disruption has measurable consequences. Ironically, this evidence base supports gut health through not doing something (avoiding unnecessary antibiotics), not through buying any product.
06
Claim by Claim: What the Evidence Actually Supports
You can test whether your gut microbiome is "healthy"
Not supported
No universal signature of a healthy microbiome exists. The 2025 Lancet Gastro consensus panel explicitly discourages direct-to-consumer testing and post-test therapeutic advice. The Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes ratio should be excluded from reports entirely.
"Dysbiosis" is a diagnosable condition that supplements can correct
Not supported
Dysbiosis is a circular, clinically vague term. Walker and Hoyles Nature Microbiology 2023 describe it as lacking scientific value in its current application. It is a marketing term, not a clinical diagnosis.
Eating more diverse plant foods benefits your gut microbiome
Directionally consistent
Large observational data including Fackelmann 2025 (n=21,561) consistently shows plant-diverse diets associate with more favorable microbial profiles. Cannot specify which foods produce which bacteria, or what health outcomes causally follow.
TMAO from red meat causes cardiovascular disease via the gut microbiome
Not causally supported
Mendelian randomization studies have not confirmed a causal TMAO-CVD link. TMAO rises with fish and omega-3 intake, which reduce CVD. The association likely reflects renal function confounding, not a gut microbiome causal pathway.
Probiotic supplements improve general gut health
Strain and condition specific
Some probiotic strains have modest RCT support for IBS symptom relief and antibiotic-associated diarrhea. General gut health claims are not supported. The strains in most commercial products are not the strains studied in RCTs.
FMT can restore gut health
Yes, for C. difficile recurrence
Multiple RCTs confirm FMT as highly effective for recurrent C. difficile infection. This is the strongest evidence that microbiome intervention causally affects clinical outcomes. For other conditions, evidence is preliminary.
Microbiome-based personalized diets are ready for clinical practice
Not ready
The field is still primarily descriptive. Companies are selling personalized diet products based on speculative associations the science cannot validate. It follows the same playbook as DNA-based personalized nutrition, which has also not been validated in RCTs.
Synthesis: Where the Science Actually Is
The gut microbiome matters. That part is not in dispute. The microbiome plays real roles in immunity, metabolism, and likely mental health through the gut-brain axis. The problem is the enormous gap between that general importance and the specific, actionable claims being sold to consumers right now.

What the field can say with confidence: Dietary diversity, especially plant food diversity, associates with greater microbial diversity. Antibiotics cause measurable, lasting disruption. Fermented foods increased both microbial diversity and reduced inflammatory markers in a controlled trial. FMT works for C. difficile. These are genuine, evidence-based findings.

What the field cannot yet say: What a healthy microbiome looks like. Which specific foods reliably produce which specific bacteria in which specific people. Whether any of those bacterial shifts causally produce health outcomes rather than simply correlating with them. No researcher, no company, and no test can tell you that your microbiome is "dysbiotic" in a clinically meaningful way, or that a specific supplement will fix it.

The TMAO lesson is instructive. A plausible mechanism, lots of observational associations, wide media coverage, and then Mendelian randomization finds no causal link in humans. The gut microbiome field is full of stories at the stage just before the MR data comes in. The research is real and evolving. The supplements are running decades ahead of it.

The honest advice: eat varied whole foods, avoid unnecessary antibiotics, eat fermented foods if you enjoy them, and treat any product claiming to "optimize your microbiome" with the same skepticism you would apply to any claim not yet validated in properly controlled human trials.
